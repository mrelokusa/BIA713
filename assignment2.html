Question 1.1 – Histograms & QQ Plots per Gender [10]

For each gender group a histogram and a QQ (Quantile-Quantile) plot were constructed for the mathematics exam scores. A histogram reveals the shape of the frequency distribution, while a QQ plot compares the observed quantiles against the theoretical quantiles of a normal distribution, if the points lie close to the diagonal reference line, the data can be considered approximately normal.

Male Students (n = 116)

Interpretation: The histogram for male students displays a fairly symmetric, bell-shaped distribution centred around a mean of approximately 69. There is no strong skew visible; the distribution appears to taper off gradually on both sides. The QQ plot confirms this, the majority of points fall very close to the red reference line with only minor deviations at the extreme tails, which is consistent with approximate normality. The Shapiro–Wilk test statistic of W = 0.9884 (p = 0.4269) supports this conclusion: since p > 0.05 we fail to reject the null hypothesis of normality.

Conclusion: The mathematics exam scores for male students are approximately normally distributed.

The Shapiro–Wilk test gives W = 0.9887 (p = 0.3418); since p > 0.05, we fail to reject the null hypothesis of normality.

Conclusion: The mathematics exam scores for female students are also approximately normally distributed, despite the modest left skew visible in the histogram.

Question 2.3 – Conclusion on Normality [4]

The Shapiro–Wilk test formally rejects normality for both provinces (WC: p = 0.0032; EC: p < 0.0001). However, this result must be interpreted carefully: the Shapiro–Wilk test is highly sensitive when n > 50, and small, practically insignificant deviations from normality can produce statistically significant results. Examining the graphical evidence and the numerical shape measures together, both distributions are only very mildly non-normal.

The skewness values for both provinces are close to zero (WC: −0.07; EC: 0.19) and the excess kurtosis values are modest (WC: 0.73; EC: 0.28). The histograms are broadly bell-shaped and the QQ plots show deviations only at the extremes. These shape measures collectively indicate that both distributions are only mildly non-normal and conform closely to a bell-shaped pattern in the central range.

Conclusion: Although the Shapiro–Wilk test formally rejects normality, the graphical and numerical evidence indicates that both the WC and EC HEADINC populations are approximately normally distributed. The deviations are minor and practically negligible. Proceeding with normal-based probability calculations is justifiable.
